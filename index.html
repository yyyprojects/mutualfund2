The Fruit Basket Analogy

If you buy one apple and it's rotten, your entire purchase is a loss. But if you buy a basket with many different fruits, one bad apple doesn't matter much. A mutual fund is a single purchase that gives you an entire basket of investments, instantly spreading your risk.

The Devastating Power of a 1% Fee

The single most important factor you can control is cost. A fee that seems tiny has a massive corrosive effect over time, because it reduces the money you have to grow in all future years. This is the magic and tragedy of compounding.

How to Get Started in 4 Steps

Taking the first step is easier than you think. Here's a simple, practical path to making your first investment.

1

Define Your Goals & Budget

What is this money for and when do you need it? Retirement in 30 years? A house in 5? Your goal determines your risk level. Then decide how much you can invest regularly.

2

Choose the Right Account

Start with a workplace 401(k) to get any employer match (it's free money!). Then consider an IRA for tax advantages. Use a standard brokerage account for other goals.

3

Select Your First Fund

For beginners, a low-cost, broadly diversified index fund (like an S&P 500 or Total Stock Market fund) is a fantastic starting point. Look for funds with an expense ratio below 0.20%.

4

Place Your Order

Enter the fund's ticker symbol (e.g., FXAIX) and the dollar amount you want to invest. Choose to "reinvest all distributions" to maximize growth. That's it, you're an investor!
